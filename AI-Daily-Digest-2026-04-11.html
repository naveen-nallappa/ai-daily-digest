Meta Unveils "Muse Spark," Its First Proprietary (Closed) AI Model

Meta debuted Muse Spark this week, the first model from its newly formed Meta Superintelligence Labs and its first major AI release since the $14B deal that brought in Alexandr Wang. Muse Spark is a natively multimodal reasoning model with tool-use, visual chain-of-thought, and multi-agent orchestration — and notably, it is a closed proprietary model, a sharp departure from Meta's Llama open-source strategy. It now powers the Meta AI app, will roll out across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, and Ray-Ban Meta glasses, and sent Meta shares surging on launch.

Why it matters: Meta's pivot to closed models signals that even the most vocal open-source proponents now see proprietary frontier models as the only way to monetize AI investment at scale. For wealth managers, this narrows the competitive field of viable enterprise AI partners and reinforces the importance of multi-vendor strategies — firms that built on Llama for data-sovereignty reasons should be reassessing whether their long-term roadmap still aligns with Meta's new direction.

Wealth managers should expect near-term regulatory guidance on AI-driven threat modeling, and CIOs should be asking their third-party security vendors whether they are in the Glasswing consortium, because defenders without Mythos-class tooling may be outmatched.

Bloomberg Government reports that lawmakers are drafting a national AI transparency standard that would require companies to disclose more about how models are built, trained, and evaluated. The effort is accelerating as the federal "innovation-first" posture under the Trump administration runs up against a growing patchwork of state-level AI rules that begin taking effect this year. Wealth managers should begin documenting model provenance, training data categories, and evaluation metrics for every AI system touching client data now — whatever Congress passes will almost certainly require it retroactively.
